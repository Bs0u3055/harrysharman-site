## The Character

Think: a sharp, warm, slightly amused British person who builds things with AI and genuinely knows how people think. Not a thought leader. Not a tech evangelist. Someone who has opinions, admits uncertainty, and talks to you like you're clever — because you are.

The site sits between a professional portfolio and a personal creative space. The tone should feel like the person, not the job title.

---

## Core Principles

**1. Get to the point, but earn it.**  
Start with something that makes the reader want the next sentence. Not a preamble, not a mission statement. A hook — personal, provocative, or just interesting.

**2. Respect intelligence, not knowledge.**  
The reader is smart. They might not know the jargon. Never talk down. Never assume acronyms. Explain things naturally — the way you'd explain them to a curious friend, not a seminar.

**3. Short sentences land harder.**  
Not always. But when making a real point: one sentence. Then let it breathe.

**4. Have a point of view.**  
Don't just describe. Interpret. "Here's what this is" is fine. "Here's what this is and why it matters to me" is better. Opinions — even uncomfortable ones — are what make someone worth reading.

**5. Be honest about uncertainty.**  
"Nobody really knows yet" is refreshing. "I might be wrong, but..." is human. The reader is drowning in confident takes — intellectual honesty goes a long way.

**6. Personal stories anchor ideas.**  
Abstract ideas stick when they're attached to something real: a conversation, a moment, a frustration. Harry's writing draws on his son, his behavioural science work, his own failures with apps. Use these. They're not distractions — they're the point.

**7. Dry wit, not jokes.**  
British understatement. The odd wry observation. Never slapstick, never try-hard. If a line makes you smile, leave it. If you're stretching for a joke, cut it.

**8. Professional but never corporate.**  
This isn't a brand deck. Don't write like one. No passive voice hedging. No "solutions" or "journeys." Real words. Real sentences. The kind you'd use talking to a smart person across a table.

## Voice Specifics

### What Harry's writing sounds like
- Em dashes — used freely for asides and emphasis
- Short paragraphs. Often single lines. For weight.
- Direct address: "you", "your brain", "look..."
- Behavioural science woven in naturally, not name-dropped
- Metaphors that are vivid and a bit unexpected ("caffeinated squirrels", "neural concrete")
- Self-deprecating humour at low volume ("I know...", "mostly", "probably")
- Ends on a thought, not a call to action

### Phrases that fit
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Which, when you think about it..."
- "Not because it's complicated — it isn't."
- "Worth paying attention to."
- "And yes, I've tried everything else."
- "Look, honestly..."
- "The bit that matters is this:"
- "Right, so."

### Words and phrases to avoid
- Game-changing / revolutionary / unprecedented
- "Let's unpack that" / "deep dive"
- "Passionate about" / "excited to share"
- Silicon Valley speak: disrupt, paradigm shift, move the needle, ecosystem
- "In this post I will explore..."
- Jargon without a natural, in-sentence explanation
- Anything that sounds like it was written by a brand committee

## For Different Page Types

**Home page (index.html)**  
Confident, warm, slightly self-aware. "This is who I am, here's what I'm doing, here's why it's interesting." Not a pitch. More like a good opening line at a dinner party.

**Project pages (unload.html, tomorrowland.html)**  
Tell a story. Start with the problem — the human frustration that made the thing worth building or writing. Features and specs come later. The emotional truth comes first.

**Blog posts**  
Most latitude. Can be angry, funny, tender, rigorous. Always opinionated. Always earned. The Beautiful Thinking series is the benchmark — smart but personal, referenced but readable.

**About section**  
Concise. No chronological CV recap. What does this person care about and why does that make them interesting? That's the question the about section answers.

## The Test

Read it aloud. Does it sound like a real person, or like a website? If you can picture someone saying it, it's probably right. If it sounds like a press release, it isn't.

If in doubt: shorter, plainer, more direct.
