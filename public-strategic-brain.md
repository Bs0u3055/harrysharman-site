# How Harry Thinks — Public Strategic Brain

This isn't a brand strategy textbook. It's how I actually think. The frameworks, the moves, the mental models. Use it as a prompt when you're stuck on a problem.

---

## 1. What Strategy Actually Is

Strategy is not a plan. It's a defensible position earned through understanding how people actually behave.

A plan describes what you'll do. Strategy explains why that's the right thing to do — given how humans *really* work, not how they wish they worked.

**The core belief:** Most strategy fails because it's built on the assumption that people are rational actors. They're not. We're irrational in predictable ways. And those predictable irrationalities are where real competitive advantage lives.

**What strategy does:**
- Produces a clear, defensible position grounded in a human truth
- Articulates what you stand for, who you're for, and why now
- Creates a decision filter — what you say yes and no to
- Builds the narrative frame that makes everything else coherent

**What strategy doesn't do:**
- Provide a slide deck framework to fill in
- Describe an audience list and call it strategy
- Create a vision statement nobody believes
- Get polished into something dead

## 2. The Insight Formula

This is the first thing you need to get right. If your insight is wrong, everything else will be wrong.

An insight has four parts:

**[Target Group] in [Context] want [Motivation] but [Conflict].**

- **Target Group:** Not broad. Specific. "Specialists who've seen treatment fail" not "doctors"
- **Context:** When does this tension surface? The relevant moment.
- **Motivation:** What do they actually want, fear, or believe?
- **Conflict:** Here's the tension — the gap between their motivation and their reality.

The conflict is what makes it an insight. Without it, you've got an observation. With it, you've got something that unlocks strategy.

**Test:** Does it make a room go quiet? Does it ring true even when it's uncomfortable?

## 3. How I Approach Any New Problem

This is my default sequence. Use it when you're stuck:

1. **Who is this actually about?** Not the obvious answer. The person behind the behaviour.
2. **What's the real tension?** Not what the brief says. What's the human conflict underneath?
3. **What does this remind me of?** Pattern-matching. What do I know from adjacent domains?
4. **What's the one most important thing?** Force a choice. Resist the urge to include everything.
5. **What would a conventional strategist do? Now don't do that.** Your competitive advantage isn't in being more thorough — it's in being different.

## 4. Three Behavioural Science Frameworks That Actually Work

### COM-B (Behaviour Change)

**Capability + Opportunity + Motivation = Behaviour**

When behaviour isn't happening, most people assume it's a capability problem (knowledge gap). It rarely is.

- **Capability:** Do they know how? Usually yes.
- **Opportunity:** Does the environment support it? (Physical and social)
- **Motivation:** Do they *want* to? (Automatic/habitual vs reflective/deliberate)

Most healthcare communication treats everything as capability ("give them more information"). But behaviour change usually happens at Opportunity and Motivation.

The practical implication: If your strategy is "communicate better," you're probably solving the wrong problem.

### Dual Process (System 1 vs System 2)

**System 1:** Fast, automatic, emotional, habitual. "The shortcut."
**System 2:** Slow, deliberate, effortful, rational. "The hard work."

Most decisions happen in System 1. Most persuasion is designed for System 2.

This explains a lot of why rational arguments fail. You're appealing to slow thinking in a moment where fast thinking decides.

### The Behavioural Virility Model

How does an idea or behaviour spread? When it hits the intersection of:
- **Social proof** (everyone around me is doing it)
- **Identity salience** (this matters to who I am)
- **Accessibility** (easy to act on)

That's when something goes viral. Watch how people adopt new treatments, health behaviours, or beliefs — it's almost never the rational argument that tips it. It's the moment when your peer does it.

## 5. Five "Micro" Concepts That Compound

### Micro-Laziness → Macro-Laziness

The small behaviour that isn't a big deal individually but compounds into something larger. Holding your breath walking past smokers instead of taking a different route. Not putting the barbell clip on at the gym where you came to do hard things. Skipping the follow-up email.

Each one costs ~1%. They compound to macro-failure.

The antidote isn't heroic effort. It's systematic small-things-done-properly.

### "F*** Future You"

Every choice to take the easy path now is a vote against your future self. Non-adherence to treatment. Delaying diagnosis. Choosing comfort over capability.

Apply this to brand strategy too: optimising for this campaign at the expense of long-term equity? You're f***ing over your future self.

### Strategic Dissonance ≠ Cognitive Dissonance

Cognitive dissonance is a psychological bug — your brain races to resolve it.

Strategic dissonance is a feature. Holding contradictory truths in tension. Patagonia says "Don't buy this jacket." Tesla links luxury and sustainability. Ben & Jerry's combines sugar and social justice.

None of these are logical. They're emotionally contradictory — deliberately designed. And they're impossible to displace because they're woven into identity.

### The Hawthorne Effect + Identity

People change behaviour when they're being measured. But the change persists when it becomes identity. "I'm the kind of person who..." is stronger than "I'm supposed to..."

This is how habit formation actually works. Initial motivation gets you started. Identity keeps you going.

### Signalling is Real

Premium is psychological, not material. The price you charge is part of what you sell. The format you choose carries meaning. The choice of channel signals something about the brand.

This isn't manipulation. It's how humans actually work.

## 6. On Insight vs. Findings

This is where most strategy fails.

**A Finding** is what the research said. "Patients are worried about side effects." True, but so what?

**An Insight** is what it means. "Patients aren't afraid of side effects themselves — they're afraid that experiencing side effects means they're failing their treatment and will have to change their identity."

The difference: a Finding is what the data says. An Insight is the human truth underneath it.

Most agency work stops at Findings and calls it strategy. That's why most strategy is forgettable.

Real insight: Makes a room quiet. Opens up possibilities rather than closing them down. Is slightly uncomfortable because it's true.

## 8. What I Notice (That Most Strategists Miss)

- **The thing people aren't saying.** The stated concern is rarely the real one. You listen for what's underneath — the identity threat, the fear, the wish not to be different.

- **When 'strategy' is actually execution.** A strategic direction that's just a tactical idea with rationale written backwards. You can smell it.

- **The HCP who doesn't believe the data.** Peer credibility, clinical experience, and social proof matter more than the RCT. Prescribing behaviour changes through narrative and social proof, not argument.

- **When the brief is asking the wrong question.** Often it diagnoses a knowledge problem when the real problem is motivation or system design.

- **The emotional territory nobody's claiming.** In a category where everyone talks efficacy and safety, there's often open emotional space. Plant the flag there.

## 9. My Failure Modes (The Honest Part)

Know these. They're real.

**Too many ideas, not enough closure.** I generate ideas easily. I sometimes let that generative moment extend into indecision. Strategy requires killing good ideas for great ones. That's a different mental mode.

**Stopping at Findings.** Easy to mistake a research finding for an insight. I catch this in my own work about 70% of the time.

**Contrarian without Storyteller.** I can spot when a brief is wrong. But if I don't build the alternative narrative that earns the right to be contrarian, I just create friction without traction.

**Impatience with execution.** Once strategy is cracked, I want to move on. Watch: mediocre strategy well-executed beats brilliant strategy poorly executed every time. The strategy isn't done when the document ships. It's done when the work is right.

**Complexity over clarity.** I see connections across domains others miss. The failure mode: including all those connections in the final output to show the richness of the thinking. The client doesn't need the workings. Just the answer.

**Overconfidence in pattern-matching.** I arrive at patterns quickly and feel confident in them. The check: does the data actually support this pattern, or am I seeing what I expect to see?

## 10. On AI, Strategy, and the Future

### AI changes the work in three ways (not one):

**Speed of execution** — Research synthesis, drafts, translations. Days → minutes. Raises the floor, not automatically the ceiling.

**Nature of advantage** — If frameworks become commoditised, advantage shifts to: quality of insight (genuine understanding), quality of relationships (trust), and proprietary IP (methodologies you own).

**New roles** — AI that can query your own work, synthesise precedents, draft first-pass documents. The human job becomes direction, curation, and judgment.

### What's actually true about AI and human thinking:

**AI creates from probability. Humans create from wonder.**

AI remixes existing patterns. It doesn't reach beyond training data toward the genuinely new. The person who can hold big-picture thinking and intuition — that's still human work.

**Behaviour change happens at the speed of psychology, not Moore's Law.**

Technology doesn't succeed at the speed of GPUs. It succeeds at the speed we change. AI adoption will crawl in areas that demand identity shifts or require trust in black boxes. Predictions of "mass adoption in two years" assume we're rational actors. We're not.

**Cognitive abundance creates cognitive obesity.**

Ultra-processed food didn't make people evil — it removed friction from eating. AI removes friction from thinking. That's a gift. The danger is default reliance. Cognitive reserve (your brain's ability to reroute around damage) builds like muscle — through load. Remove the load, quietly erode resilience you'll want decades later.
