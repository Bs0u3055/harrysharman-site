Anthropic just bought the dev tools company that OpenAI, Google, and Cloudflare all use. Elon Musk lost his lawsuit against Sam Altman. And drug discovery AI just got a lot easier to use. Here's what happened.

So, Anthropic acquired Stainless — a two-and-a-half-year-old New York startup that automates the creation and maintenance of software development kits. SDKs are the libraries developers use to interact with APIs, and until now, keeping them updated has been tedious, manual work. Stainless made that automatic. And here's the bit that matters: OpenAI, Google, and Cloudflare were all Stainless customers. Anthropic just bought the tooling that powers its competitors' developer experience.

Why does that matter? Because developer adoption is the quiet battleground in AI. Models matter, sure. But if your SDK is clunky, slow to update, or breaks when you ship a new feature, developers will go elsewhere. Anthropic's bet here is that owning the plumbing — the boring infrastructure that makes integration painless — gives them a structural advantage. It's not flashy. But it's smart. And it's the kind of move that pays off over years, not quarters.

Watch for whether Anthropic keeps serving OpenAI and Google or quietly makes Stainless exclusive. That'll tell you whether this was a defensive acquisition or an offensive one.

Meanwhile, Elon Musk lost his lawsuit against Sam Altman and OpenAI. After nearly a month of testimony, a jury in California took about two hours to return a unanimous verdict in OpenAI's favour. Musk's central claim — that Altman and OpenAI had betrayed the founding mission by shifting focus from benefiting humanity to boosting profits — failed on statute of limitations grounds. Two of his claims were filed too late, and the third collapsed as a result.

Now, here's the thing. The trial itself was less interesting for the legal outcome than for what it revealed about the people running the AI industry. Both sides came out looking... let's say, less than inspiring. Musk's lawyers painted Altman as profit-driven and ethically flexible. Altman's lawyers poked at Musk's credibility and motives. The Verge put it well: the trial proved that AI is led by the wrong people. And honestly, it's hard to argue.

For the rest of us, the takeaway is simple. The future of AI is being shaped by personality clashes, ego, and courtroom drama as much as by technical progress. That's not reassuring. But it is clarifying.

And finally, a practical one. SandboxAQ — a company spun out of Alphabet that builds AI models for drug discovery — just made those models available directly through Claude. No PhD in computing required. You describe what you're trying to discover, Claude talks to SandboxAQ's models, and you get results. The interface is conversational. The complexity is hidden.

Why does that matter? Because until now, the biggest obstacle in drug discovery AI wasn't the quality of the models. It was access. You needed specialist knowledge, infrastructure, and time just to run a query. SandboxAQ is betting that the limiting factor isn't capability — it's usability. And if they're right, then suddenly a much wider group of researchers, clinicians, and small biotech firms can start using this stuff.

It's early days. But this is the pattern to watch: AI gets better when it gets easier. The companies that win won't just have the best models. They'll have the simplest interface.
